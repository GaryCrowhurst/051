üßπ Housekeeping Section

üéØ What is Good Housekeeping?

Good housekeeping means keeping the workshop clean, tidy, organised, and safe. It prevents accidents, improves efficiency, creates a professional image, and makes the workplace more pleasant for everyone.

Why Good Housekeeping Matters

Safety: Reduces slips, trips, and falls - the most common workshop accidents
Efficiency: Easy to find tools and equipment, saves time searching
Professional Image: Customers trust a clean, organised workshop
Equipment Life: Properly maintained tools last longer and work better
Fire Prevention: Removing combustible waste reduces fire risk
Health: Clean environment reduces dust, fumes, and skin problems
Morale: People work better in a clean, pleasant environment

üîß Topic 1: Tools and Equipment Maintenance

üéØ Key Understanding: Regular checking and cleaning of tools and equipment ensures they work safely and efficiently. Poorly maintained tools can cause accidents, damage vehicles, and waste time.

Six Essential Tools and Equipment

üîß
1. Spanners & Sockets
Checks:
  • No cracks or damage
  • Jaws not worn or spread
  • Chrome not chipped
  • Socket drive not worn
Cleaning:
  • Wipe with clean rag after use
  • Remove oil and grease
  • Light oil to prevent rust
  • Store in tool chest organised

  • Safety locks function
  • No damaged arms/pads
  • Control buttons work

  • Clean lifting pads
  • Sweep underneath
  • Check fluid levels weekly

  • Screen clear and working
  • Software up to date
  • Battery charged
  • Connectors clean

  • Wipe screen with soft cloth
  • Clean connectors carefully
  • Remove dust from vents
  • Store in protective case

  • Guards fitted and secure
  • Trigger/switch works safely
  • Air hose no leaks (if pneumatic)

  • Wipe casing clean

  • No bent or damaged parts

  • Faulty equipment can damage vehicles
  • Unsafe power tools can cause electric shocks or fires
  • Uncalibrated torque wrenches cause fastener failure

üìÖ Topic 2: Daily Workshop Cleaning Activities

üéØ Key Understanding: Consistent daily cleaning prevents the build-up of dirt, waste, and hazards. A clean start and finish to each day maintains safety standards and work efficiency.

Start of Day Activities

‚òÄÔ∏è Morning Checklist
Inspect Workshop: Walk through and identify any hazards or spills from previous day
Check Lighting: Ensure all lights working, replace any blown bulbs
Clear Walkways: Remove any obstacles, tools, or equipment blocking access
Empty Bins: Check waste bins aren't overflowing, empty if necessary
Check Fire Exits: Ensure all fire exits clear and accessible
Sweep Main Areas: Quick sweep of high-traffic areas
Tool Check: Verify tools are in correct locations and ready for use
Ventilation: Turn on extraction fans and ensure airflow

End of Day Activities

üåô Evening Checklist
Return Tools: Put all tools back in correct storage locations
Clean Work Benches: Wipe down benches, remove parts and debris
Sweep Floors: Thorough sweep of entire workshop floor
Clean Spillages: Mop up any oil, coolant, or fluid spills
Dispose Waste: Empty waste bins into correct recycling/disposal containers
Store Parts: Put unused parts back in parts department or secure storage
Coil Hoses/Cables: Tidy air lines, electric cables, avoid trip hazards
Secure Equipment: Lower ramps, turn off equipment, lock tool cabinets
Turn Off Lights: Switch off all unnecessary lighting and equipment
Lock Up: Secure windows, doors, and set alarm if applicable

Floor Cleaning Methods

Different Spillages Require Different Methods:

Oil and Fuel Spillages

Step 1: Sprinkle absorbent granules (sawdust, cat litter, or specialist absorbent)
Step 2: Allow to soak in for 10-15 minutes
Step 3: Sweep up saturated granules into waste oil container
Step 4: Clean remaining residue with degreaser or detergent
Step 5: Mop with clean water and allow to dry

Coolant and Water-Based Fluids

Method: Mop up immediately with absorbent cloth or mop
Cleaning: Use floor cleaner and warm water
Drying: Dry with clean mop or allow to air dry with "Wet Floor" sign displayed

General Workshop Floor

Daily: Sweep with broom to remove dirt, dust, and metal filings
Weekly: Wet mop with industrial floor cleaner
As Needed: Use floor scrubber machine for deep cleaning
Drains: Clear drain grids regularly to prevent blockages
üö® Never:
  • Hose oil spills down drains - it's illegal and harms the environment
  • Leave wet floors without warning signs
  • Mix chemicals when cleaning - can create toxic fumes
  • Use water on electrical equipment or near electrical outlets

Metal Waste

What Goes Here: Steel components, aluminium parts, copper wire, brake discs/drums, exhaust systems, metal offcuts

Policy:

  • Collect in designated metal skip or bin
  • Separate ferrous (magnetic) from non-ferrous metals if required
  • Remove any attached rubber, plastic, or oil before disposal
  • Collected by licensed scrap metal merchant

Your Responsibilities:

  • Place metal waste in correct bin - don't mix with general waste
  • Drain oil from components before placing in metal bin
  • Stack neatly to maximise bin space
  • Report when metal bin is full

Environmental Benefit: Recycling metal saves energy and raw materials. For example, recycling aluminium uses 95% less energy than producing new aluminium.

Cardboard Waste

What Goes Here: Cardboard boxes, packaging, parts boxes, clean cardboard tubes

Policy:

  • Collect in cardboard recycling bin or designated area
  • Flatten boxes to save space
  • Remove tape and staples if possible
  • Keep dry - wet cardboard can't be recycled
  • Collected by recycling company fortnightly/monthly

Your Responsibilities:

  • Break down boxes immediately after unpacking parts
  • Don't put oily or contaminated cardboard in recycling
  • Place in correct bin, not mixed with general waste
  • Report if recycling bin is full

Environmental Benefit: Recycling cardboard saves trees and reduces landfill waste. One tonne of recycled cardboard saves 17 trees.

Oils Waste

What Goes Here: Engine oil, gear oil, brake fluid, power steering fluid, oil filters

Policy:

  • Collect in dedicated waste oil storage tank (usually bunded)
  • Oil filters drain for 12 hours then stored in separate container
  • Oil-soaked absorbent materials in contaminated waste bin
  • NEVER mix with coolant, solvents, or water
  • Collected by licensed waste carrier with Waste Transfer Notes

Your Responsibilities:

  • Drain oil carefully into collection pan
  • Transfer to waste oil tank using funnel to prevent spills
  • Puncture and drain oil filters before storage
  • Wear gloves - used oil is carcinogenic
  • Report any leaks from waste oil tank immediately
  • Keep spill kit nearby when handling waste oil

Legal Requirement: Used oil is classified as hazardous waste. Improper disposal can result in fines up to ¬£50,000 and imprisonment.

Environmental Impact: Just 1 litre of oil can contaminate 1 million litres of water. Proper disposal is critical.

Plastic Waste

What Goes Here: Plastic bottles, oil containers, screen wash bottles, plastic packaging, bubble wrap

Policy:

  • Collect in plastic recycling bin
  • Rinse containers to remove residue if possible
  • Remove lids (different plastic type)
  • Crush bottles to save space
  • Collected by recycling company

Your Responsibilities:

  • Don't put oily plastic in recycling - goes in general waste
  • Separate clean plastic from contaminated plastic
  • Place in correct recycling bin
  • Empty containers before disposal

Environmental Benefit: Plastic takes 450+ years to decompose. Recycling reduces ocean pollution and landfill.

Three Essential Housekeeping Rules

üìú Workshop Housekeeping Rules
RULE 1: Clean As You Go

Don't leave cleaning until the end of the day. Wipe up spills immediately, return tools after use, and dispose of waste as it's created. This prevents accidents and keeps work flowing smoothly.

RULE 2: Everything Has a Place

Every tool, part, and piece of equipment must have a designated storage location. Use shadow boards, tool chests, and labelled storage. "A place for everything and everything in its place."

RULE 3: Report Hazards Immediately

If you see a hazard you can't fix yourself (leak, broken equipment, blocked fire exit), report it to your supervisor straight away. Don't assume someone else will deal with it.

üéØ What is Health and Safety?

Health and Safety at Work is about preventing accidents, injuries, and ill-health in the workplace. Everyone - employers AND employees - has legal duties under the Health and Safety at Work Act 1974.

The Health & Safety at Work Act 1974

‚öñÔ∏è Key Legal Duties

Employer's Duties:

Ensure health, safety, and welfare of all employees
Provide safe equipment and safe systems of work
Provide information, instruction, training, and supervision
Maintain workplace in safe condition
Provide adequate welfare facilities

Employee's Duties:

Take reasonable care for your own health and safety
Take care not to endanger others by your actions
Co-operate with your employer on health and safety matters
Not interfere with or misuse safety equipment
Report hazards, near-misses, and accidents

Three Main Health and Safety Issues

1. Slips, Trips, and Falls

The Problem: Most common cause of workplace injury. Oil spills, trailing cables, items left on floor, uneven surfaces.

Prevention:

  • Clean spills immediately
  • Use cable management/overhead hose reels
  • Keep walkways clear
  • Wear appropriate footwear
  • Use warning signs for wet floors
2. Manual Handling Injuries

The Problem: Back injuries, muscle strains, trapped fingers from lifting heavy parts, batteries, wheels, etc.

Prevention:

  • Use lifting equipment (engine crane, trolley jacks)
  • Get help with heavy items - don't lift alone
  • Bend knees, keep back straight when lifting
  • Assess weight before attempting to lift
  • Attend manual handling training
3. Hazardous Substances

The Problem: Skin damage, breathing problems, poisoning from brake cleaner, petrol, battery acid, coolant, solvents.

Prevention:

  • Read COSHH data sheets
  • Wear appropriate PPE (gloves, goggles, respirator)
  • Use in ventilated areas
  • Store chemicals safely in correct containers
  • Never eat or drink near chemicals

Three Examples Where Caution Required

1. Working on Hybrid/Electric Vehicles

Danger: High voltage (up to 600V) can cause fatal electric shock

Cautions:

  • Only qualified technicians can work on high-voltage systems
  • Identify vehicle type before starting work
  • Look for high-voltage warning labels (orange cables)
  • Isolate high-voltage battery before any work
  • Use insulated tools rated for high voltage
  • Wear appropriate PPE including insulated gloves
2. Working with Airbags

Danger: Accidental deployment can cause serious injury or death

Cautions:

  • Disconnect battery and wait 10 minutes before working on airbags
  • Never probe airbag with test equipment
  • Never attempt to repair airbag - replace only
  • Point airbag away from you when handling
  • Store in secure place face-up
  • Follow manufacturer procedures exactly
3. Brake System Repairs

Danger: Brake failure can cause fatal accidents

Cautions:

  • Use correct brake fluid - never mix types
  • Bleed brakes properly to remove all air
  • Torque wheel nuts to correct specification
  • Test brakes thoroughly before releasing vehicle
  • Check brake fluid reservoir before test drive
  • Document all work in case of future investigation

Fire Safety Actions

üö® If You Discover a Fire:
  1. RAISE THE ALARM - Sound fire alarm or shout "FIRE!"
  2. CALL 999 - Don't assume someone else has done it
  3. EVACUATE - Leave building by nearest safe exit
  4. ASSEMBLE - Go to designated assembly point
  5. STAY OUT - Never re-enter until fire service say it's safe

Only attempt to fight fire if:

  • It's small and contained
  • You've been trained
  • You have the right extinguisher
  • You have a safe escape route

Personal Protective Equipment is worn to reduce exposure to hazards that could cause injury or illness. PPE should be the last line of defence - always try to eliminate hazards first.
